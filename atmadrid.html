History of Atletico Madrid

Atlético Madrid: A Comprehensive History from Its Foundation to 2025 Foundation and Early Years (1903–1930s) Club Atlético de Madrid, commonly known as Atlético Madrid, was founded on April 26, 1903, by three Basque students living in Madrid. Originally named Athletic Club de Madrid, the club was established as a youth branch of Athletic Club Bilbao. The red-and-white striped kits, adopted in 1911, became the club's iconic look and earned them the nickname "Los Colchoneros" (The Mattress Makers), as the colors resembled traditional Spanish mattress covers. In 1921, Atlético became an independent entity from Athletic Club Bilbao. The club joined the newly-formed La Liga in 1928 but struggled to compete with established teams like Real Madrid and Barcelona in its early years. The First Golden Era and Early Titles (1940s–1950s) Atlético’s fortunes changed in the 1940s under the guidance of coach Ricardo Zamora, a legendary Spanish goalkeeper. The club won its first La Liga title in the 1939–40 season, followed by another championship in 1941. These early successes established Atlético as one of Spain's top clubs. The 1950s marked another high point for the club. Atlético won two more La Liga titles (1950 and 1951) and began to challenge the dominance of Real Madrid. During this period, Atlético also developed a fierce rivalry with its cross-town rivals, solidifying the Madrid Derby as one of the most passionate fixtures in world football. European Beginnings and Continued Success (1960s–1970s) The 1960s saw Atlético Madrid make its mark on European football. Under the management of Domingo Balmanya and later Luis Aragonés, Atlético reached the 1962 European Cup Winners' Cup final, defeating Fiorentina to claim its first European trophy. The 1970s were a particularly successful decade for the club. Atlético won La Liga titles in 1970, 1973, and 1977 and reached the European Cup final in 1974. Although they lost to Bayern Munich in a replay after the first match ended in a draw, this achievement underscored Atlético’s growing prominence on the European stage. Challenges and Resilience (1980s–1990s) The 1980s were a turbulent period for Atlético Madrid. Despite winning several Copa del Rey titles, the club struggled to maintain consistency in La Liga and faced financial difficulties. In 1987, the club was purchased by Jesús Gil, whose controversial leadership brought both challenges and occasional success. The 1990s saw Atlético enjoy a brief resurgence. Under coach Radomir Antić, Atlético achieved a historic domestic double in the 1995–96 season, winning both La Liga and the Copa del Rey. Players like Diego Simeone, Kiko Narváez, and Milan Jovanović played crucial roles in this triumph. However, the club faced a shocking relegation to Segunda División in 2000. This period marked one of the lowest points in Atlético’s history, but the club’s passionate fanbase remained loyal, and the team returned to La Liga in 2002. The Simeone Revolution and Modern Dominance (2010s–2020s) The appointment of Diego Simeone as head coach in 2011 marked a transformative period for Atlético Madrid. Known for his passionate and disciplined approach, Simeone built a team renowned for its defensive solidity, work ethic, and counterattacking prowess. Under his leadership, Atlético became a force to be reckoned with in both domestic and European competitions. 2013–14 Season: Atlético won its first La Liga title in 18 years, breaking the duopoly of Real Madrid and Barcelona. The club also reached the Champions League final, losing to Real Madrid in a dramatic extra-time defeat. 2015–16 and 2019–20 Seasons: Atlético continued to challenge for titles, reaching another Champions League final in 2016 (again losing to Real Madrid) and winning a second La Liga title under Simeone in 2020. European Success: Atlético won the Europa League in 2012 and 2018, cementing its status as one of Europe’s elite clubs. Players like Antoine Griezmann, Jan Oblak, and Koke became synonymous with the club’s success. Challenges and Adaptation (2020–2025) The 2020s brought new challenges as Atlético sought to adapt to an increasingly competitive football landscape. Despite the departure of key players like Griezmann (later returning in 2022), Atlético continued to compete at the highest level, consistently finishing in the top three of La Liga and making deep runs in European competitions. Young talents like João Félix and experienced stalwarts like Luis Suárez (who played a crucial role in the 2020–21 title-winning season) helped the club maintain its competitive edge. Simeone’s tactical adaptability and leadership ensured Atlético remained a formidable opponent for any team. Looking Ahead to 2025 As of 2025, Atlético Madrid remains one of the most respected clubs in world football. With its strong emphasis on teamwork, tactical discipline, and a passionate fanbase, the club continues to challenge for major titles in Spain and Europe. The construction of the Wanda Metropolitano Stadium in 2017 provided Atlético with a state-of-the-art home, reflecting the club’s ambitions for sustained success. The legacy of Diego Simeone, now one of the longest-serving managers in football history, remains deeply ingrained in the club's identity. With a mix of experienced players and emerging stars, Atlético Madrid looks forward to a bright future, driven by its motto: "Nunca dejes de creer" ("Never stop believing").
